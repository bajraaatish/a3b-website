Premium clients pay 3–5× more to escape this aesthetic. Naming it explicitly in pitches accelerates client recognition.

## Slide 02 — Pricing landscape: hourly rates, 2026

The global market for freelance video editing has stabilised into four tiers. Numbers below are USD, sourced from ZipRecruiter, Glassdoor, goLance, Cutjamm 2025 Rates Survey, Krock.io, and SideStackers.

| Tier | Experience | Hourly Rate | Annual Equivalent |
|------|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Junior / entry** | 0–2 years | $20–45 | ~$23,000–47,000 |
| **Mid-level** | 2–5 years | $40–85 | ~$48,000–82,000 |
| **Senior** | 5–10 years | $85–150 | ~$82,000–130,000 |
| **Top-tier / specialist** | 10+ years, niche | $150–220+ | $130,000+ |
| **Agencies** | (n/a) | $150–300 | (team rates) |

**Reference points worth holding in mind:**

- US national average freelance editor: **$29–31/hr** (Glassdoor & ZipRecruiter, 2026)
- Mid-level survey average (Cutjamm 2025 survey of working editors): **$40–80/hr**
- B2B SaaS specialist freelancer ceiling: **$75–150/hr**
- LATAM editors typically charge **30–50% less** than equivalent US/AU/UK editors
- New York / Los Angeles editors charge **20–40% more** than mid-sized US cities

The hourly average masks a wide range. The 25th–75th percentile in the US runs from $44,500 to $82,500 per year — meaning two equally-skilled editors can earn 2× each other based on positioning, niche, and pitch quality alone.

## Slide 03 — Pricing landscape: project-based rates, 2026

Most experienced editors quote per project, not per hour. The reason is documented in multiple sources: hourly billing penalises speed, invites scope creep, and undervalues complex short-form work. Project-based pricing also reads more confidently to premium clients.

### By project size

| Project type | Typical range |
|--------------|---------------|
| Short (social clips, quick promos, 15–60s) | **$100–500** |
| Medium (corporate, YouTube content, explainers, 2–10min) | **$500–2,500** |
| Large (ads, short films, multi-deliverable campaigns) | **$2,500–7,500+** |
| Wedding video editing (per event) | **$1,500–5,000** |
| Real estate (per finished minute) | **$100–150** |
| Corporate training (per finished minute) | **$150–250** |
| YouTube video (per video) | **$200–1,500** |

### Subscription / retainer models (the holy grail)

The fastest-growing pricing model in 2026, especially for content creators and SaaS clients:

- **Subscription services:** flat monthly fee for set deliverables — e.g. $1,900/month for ~20 videos = **$95/video** at high volume
- **Retainer with dedicated editor:** $2,000–5,000/month for unlimited revisions and priority turnaround
- **B2B SaaS Creative-as-a-Service:** ~$3,000/month flat rate, 24–48hr turnaround
- **Traditional B2B SaaS agency:** $4,000–20,000 per project

For a3b: retainers should be the long-term target. The first 3–6 months will mostly be project work to build trust, then transition repeat clients onto monthly retainers.

## Slide 04 — What pushes rates up (the premium levers)

The difference between a $40/hr editor and a $120/hr editor is rarely raw skill. It's positioning, evidence, and demand drivers. Six specific levers that move you up the rate ladder:

1. **Recognisable brand portfolio.** Editing for Fortune 500 clients, viral campaigns, or TV networks adds a defensible premium. Even one good logo on a reel changes pricing power immediately.
2. **Specialist skills.** Motion graphics in After Effects, Cinema 4D, advanced DaVinci Resolve grading, and sound design are all premium. Basic Premiere cuts are a commodity.
3. **Rush delivery.** 24–48hr turnaround typically adds **25–50%** to the project rate. Premium clients pay for speed.
4. **Niche specialisation.** "Real estate video editor for premium architects" outcharges "general video editor" by 30–80%. Specificity raises perceived value and reduces competition.
5. **Documented outcomes.** Case studies showing measurable results (lift in conversion, view-through rate, lead generation) command 30–80% more than portfolio-only editors.
6. **Brand polish around the service itself.** A considered website, clean invoice, on-brand client communications all push the perceived tier upward without changing the work. This is precisely where a3b has an unfair advantage — the brand is already premium.

a3b should consciously stack levers 4, 5, and 6 from day one. Specialisation (calm/minimal niche), outcomes (track results from the first project), and brand polish are all free to deploy.

## Slide 05 — Where a3b should price (recommendation)

Aatish is 19, in Australia, transitioning from minimum-wage employment with a two-month financial deadline. The pricing recommendation has to balance three competing pressures: build evidence quickly (lower rates attract more first clients), price defensively (too low attracts the wrong clients), and reach a viable income threshold to leave the day job.

### Recommended pricing ladder

| Phase | Timeline | Hourly equivalent | Project range | Goal |
|-------|----------|------------------|---------------|------|
| **Months 1–2 (proof)** | Now → first 5 clients | $30–40/hr equivalent | $300–800/project | Speed of evidence > price. Lock 5 testimonials. |
| **Months 3–4 (calibration)** | After first 5 testimonials | $45–60/hr equivalent | $600–1,500/project | Raise rates 50% once social proof exists. |
| **Months 5–6 (transition)** | After 10+ projects | $60–80/hr equivalent | $1,000–2,500/project | Approach the mid-tier global benchmark. Begin retainer conversations. |
| **Month 7+ (retainer)** | After demonstrated retention | $2,000–4,000/month per client | Retainer-led | Three retainers replace day job. |

### Two tactical rules

- **Never quote hourly to clients.** Quote per project with clear deliverables. Internally, track your hours to make sure your effective rate stays above $35/hr in phase one.
- **Always raise rates after social proof, never after time.** A new testimonial earns a price increase. Calendar time does not.

## Slide 06 — Client acquisition: what actually works

Documented strategies, ranked by evidence of conversion.

### Tier 1 — Highest conversion (the custom sample edit)

The single highest-converting pitch in 2026, by case study volume, is the **value-first custom sample edit delivered directly**.

The mechanic:
1. Deep-research a specific prospect — their existing content, their pain points, their audience
2. Create a 15–30 second custom edit using their actual footage (or a relevant equivalent)
3. Deliver one of two ways:
   - Upload as Instagram Story using **Close Friends** option, send story directly to the prospect's DMs
   - Record a personalised Loom video walking through the edit, DM via X or Instagram with the link
4. No price ask, no pitch deck, no "are you looking for an editor" — just the work and a line about why you made it

**Documented outcome:** An editor on Reddit reported securing a $2,000/month retainer after a single warm pitch using this exact method, after researching the client's channel and identifying a relevant keyword gap.

For a3b specifically, this method is unusually well-suited because the calm/minimal aesthetic is *visible* in 30 seconds — you don't need to explain it, you can demonstrate it.

### Tier 2 — Strong (Instagram warm-up + DM)

Used by hundreds of working freelancers, less documented per-case but consistent in performance:

1. Identify a target prospect (small business, real estate agent, content creator in your aesthetic neighbourhood)
2. Like 3–5 of their recent posts genuinely
3. Leave one substantive comment (never "nice post" — say something about the actual content)
4. Follow them
5. Wait 5–10 minutes
6. Send a short DM (max 3 sentences) referencing their work specifically and offering something valuable (audit, sample, idea — not a service pitch)

The warm-up makes your name visible to them in notifications before the DM lands. Conversion rates from warm-pitched DMs vs cold DMs differ by an order of magnitude.

### Tier 3 — Platforms (Upwork, Fiverr) — short-term only

Useful for building social proof and managing cash flow in months 1–3. Should not be a long-term strategy. Documented best-practice: build reviews on platform, then move clients off-platform after the second engagement to avoid the 5–10% platform fees.

### Tier 4 — Inbound / SEO / portfolio site

Slow but compounding. Worth setting up the a3b website with a clean portfolio and SEO-targeted copy ("calm video editing," "minimal video editor," "[city] real estate video editor") — but don't wait for inbound to drive your first 5 clients. Outbound dominates the first six months.

### Channels by client type

| Client type | Best channel | Why |
|-------------|--------------|-----|
| Local small businesses | LinkedIn DM + in-person meetings | Trust over reach |
| Real estate agents | Instagram DM with property-specific sample | They live on Instagram |
| Content creators / influencers | Custom edit via Close Friends Story | Standard playbook for this niche |
| SaaS / tech brands | LinkedIn + Loom + portfolio website | They expect professionalism over personality |
| Boutique hospitality / architects | Curated email with link to single best case study | They respect restraint |
| Founder-led origin-story brands | Referral via existing portfolio clients | This world runs on intros |

### First 90 days for a3b — concrete plan

- **Week 1–2:** Send 5 custom sample edits per week to specific prospects (target: 40 over 8 weeks)
- **Week 3+:** Begin Instagram warm-up sequence with 10 new prospects per week
- **Week 4+:** Post one finished piece per week to your own Instagram/LinkedIn as inbound signal
- **Goal by week 8:** First 3 paying projects landed, regardless of price (build evidence)
- **Goal by week 12:** First retainer conversation opened with a client who's done 2+ projects with you

## Slide 07 — Anti-references: what a3b is explicitly not

Premium positioning is built as much by what you reject as by what you embrace. a3b's anti-aesthetic — the work that should never come out of this brand — has a name and a market.

### The over-edited / "high-retention" aesthetic

The dominant style on TikTok, Instagram Reels, and "growth-optimised" YouTube. Visual signatures:

- **Sub-1-second average shot length** (cuts faster than the eye can register intent)
- **Stacked transitions:** whip pans, zoom-punches, mirror flips, glitch wipes, page curls
- **Effects piled on every cut:** glow, drop shadow, 3D pop, color flash, scale-bounce
- **Constant on-screen text** with high-contrast colors, drop shadows, neon outlines
- **CapCut and After Effects template packs** applied without discrimination
- **AI-generated transitions** used as decoration rather than meaning
- **Emoji overlays, arrow pointers, "hook" graphics** in the first 3 seconds
- **Yellow-circle thumbnails** with shocked faces and clickbait copy
- **Multi-color animated subtitles** that compete with the speaker

### Specific reference points (the work that ISN'T a3b)

- **MrBeast-style retention edits** — fast cuts engineered for the algorithm, not the viewer
- **Ali Abdaal-style productivity content** — clean but rapid, more energy than calm
- **CapCut template TikToks** — the algorithmic baseline aesthetic
- **Gaming highlight reels** — energy-maximalist by design
- **"Sigma edit" / gym motivation videos** — color-flash, slow-mo, dramatic music swells
- **"Day in the life" sped-up edits** — fast pacing prioritised over moments

These styles aren't wrong — they work for what they're optimised for (attention, retention, virality). They're just not what a3b makes. Saying that explicitly clarifies positioning faster than describing the calm aesthetic alone.

### Why naming the anti-aesthetic is a positioning weapon

Three reasons it matters commercially:

1. **Premium clients are actively fatigued by the loud aesthetic.** They've seen it, they've tried it, they associate it with cheap. Naming it as the contrast lets a3b stand for the relief.
2. **It clarifies the pitch in 5 seconds.** "We don't do CapCut transitions and zoom-bounces — we make calm, considered, documentary-style edits" is faster than three paragraphs about quiet computing.
3. **It pre-qualifies clients.** Clients who want loud editing will self-select out, saving everyone time. Clients who want calm will recognise themselves immediately.

a3b's website, pitch deck, and first DM all benefit from naming the contrast explicitly in at least one sentence each.

This deck closes the pricing and acquisition gaps from the original slide 10. Three open questions remain:

1. **Australian-specific market sizing.** Most pricing data is US-centric (with global commentary). Australian rates generally track US/UK at 80–100%, but verifying actual Melbourne/Sydney/Brisbane benchmarks would let a3b price with more confidence. Worth a focused Australia-only research pass when capacity allows.
2. **Aesthetic-specific pricing premium.** Numbers above are for "general video editing." Editors working specifically in the calm/minimal/luxury aesthetic may price 30–80% higher per the premium-lever data, but I couldn't find clean benchmarks for the niche. Worth gathering 5–10 specific price points from comparable editors before locking long-term pricing strategy.
