It is worth being direct about the work that should never come out of
a3b. Naming the opposite clarifies the brief faster than three paragraphs
describing calm video ever could.

## The loud aesthetic

There is a dominant style on TikTok, Reels, and growth-optimised YouTube.
Its signatures are easy to list:

- Sub-second average shot length — cuts faster than the eye registers
  intent.
- Stacked transitions: whip pans, zoom-punches, glitch wipes, page curls.
- An effect on every cut — glow, drop shadow, scale-bounce, colour flash.
- Constant on-screen text, high-contrast, often neon-outlined.
- Template packs applied without discrimination.

This work is not wrong. It is optimised for something — attention,
retention, virality — and it often hits that target. It is just not what
a3b makes, and it is not a setting a3b can turn up on request.

## Why saying so is useful

Three reasons it helps to name the contrast plainly.

**Premium clients are fatigued by the loud aesthetic.** They have seen
it, tried it, and come to associate it with cheap. Naming it as the
contrast lets a3b stand for the relief.

**It clarifies the pitch in five seconds.** "We do not do template
transitions and zoom-bounces — we make calm, documentary-style edits" is
faster than any amount of describing.

**It pre-qualifies the work.** A brief that wants loud editing
self-selects out, which saves everyone time. A brief that wants calm
recognises itself immediately.

## The honest version

a3b is small, deliberate, and narrow on purpose. If a project needs the
loud aesthetic, the right answer is to say so plainly and point it
elsewhere. A good fit is worth more than a booked job — and saying that
out loud is part of the brand, not a disclaimer on it.
