If there is one habit that separates calm video from the loud kind, it is
where the cut lands.

The default — the thing most editing tutorials teach — is to cut on
action. Something moves, you cut. A hand reaches, a head turns, a word
starts. It keeps energy high and the timeline busy. It is also exhausting
to watch for more than a minute.

a3b cuts on **stillness**.

## What that means in practice

A cut lands in the quiet moment: the breath after a sentence, the beat
after a gesture finishes, the half-second where nothing is happening yet.
The viewer's eye gets to rest, and then it is moved — with intent, not
reflex.

It feels wrong at first. Holding a shot past the point of comfort goes
against every instinct trained by fast content. But the discomfort is
short, and what it buys is attention. A held shot says: *this is worth
looking at.* A rushed one says: *do not get attached.*

## A few working rules

- **Minimum shot length, ~1.2 seconds.** Anything shorter is a flash, not
  a frame.
- **Cut after the moment, not into the next one.** Let the line land
  before you move.
- **If a scene is doing two things, it is two scenes.** Stillness needs
  room; one idea per shot gives it room.
- **Hard cuts and the occasional cross-dissolve. Nothing else.** No
  wipes, no zooms, no spins.

None of this is technically difficult. That is the point. The skill is
not in the move — it is in the restraint to not make one.
